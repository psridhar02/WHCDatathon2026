The Problem

What happens when communities keep asking
for the same things year after year?

New York City's People's Money Program gives residents a rare opportunity: the chance to tell their government exactly what their neighborhoods need, and propose how public funds should be spent to fix it. When analyzed through the lens of Social Determinants of Health: the conditions in which people live, work, and age. These proposals don't just reveal policy gaps, they reveal a city in which some neighborhoods carry a burden that never lifts.

Our team used Python to parse three years of People's Money data, tagging 5,367 proposals across three SDOH domains: Economic Stability, Healthcare Access & Quality, and Neighborhood & Built Environment. The patterns that emerged are not subtle.

The same neighborhoods. The same needs. Three years running.

A Day in Rosa's Life

Every day is a negotiation
between need and survival.

7:00 AM
Rosa wakes up in her Bushwick apartment. The nearest grocery store closed last year, the bodega around the corner doesn't carry fresh produce.
10:00 AM
She needs to see her doctor across town. The bus stop curb cuts are broken and her walker gets stuck every single time. She's filed three complaints.

The Data

Three domains & Consistent stories of unmet needs.

01
Economic Stability

Food, housing, and financial security: the foundations of health

Economic instability is a foundational driver of poor health. Access to nutritious food, stable housing, and financial security are prerequisites for overall well-being, yet they remain out of reach for thousands of New Yorkers.

Many people like Rosa are in this data. Food insecurity, housing instability, and lack of financial resources compound to create cycles of poor health that are extremely difficult to escape.

3× Surge in Economic proposals from Year 1 to Year 3: the community's cry for help is growing louder.

*Year 2's data excludes social services, education, civic engagement, and arts & culture, explaining the dip.

02
Healthcare Access & Quality

A system built for some, out of reach for many.

Approximately 1 in 10 Americans lacks health insurance, leading to delayed treatment and worsening chronic conditions. The barriers fall hardest on older adults (like Rosa), geographic distance from providers, unaffordable copayments, and confusing enrollment processes.

10× Jamaica submitted the highest healthcare proposals of any single neighborhood in Year 1: a signal impossible to ignore.
Top Healthcare Proposals by Neighborhood (Year 1–3)
03
Neighborhood & Built Environment

Where you live shapes how long you live.

The physical environment profoundly affects health behaviors, safety, and mobility. Many low-income neighborhoods lack wheelchair-accessible transit, well-maintained sidewalks, adequate lighting, and fall-prevention infrastructure.

According to Healthy People 2030, fall-related deaths are highest among adults 85+. Rosa's broken curb cuts aren't a minor inconvenience, they are a life-threatening infrastructure failure.

67 Built environment proposals from Hunts Point & Longwood alone, the highest of any neighborhood.

3-Year Trend

Voices growing louder.
Needs remain unmet.

Across all three years of the People's Money program, community engagement has not just persisted, it has surged. The number of proposals nearly doubled from Year 1 to Year 3, with the steepest growth in Economic Stability needs.

The Year 2 dip is a reflection of a smaller dataset, not less need. The community kept showing up. The question is whether the city is listening.

What Must Change

Three domains. Three directives.
One urgent call.

Effective policy must match the nature of this burden. Addressing housing without healthcare, or healthcare without economic security, is not enough. A coordinated, cross-domain investment is both a moral imperative and a practical necessity.

01
Economic Stability

Expand Food & Economic Security

  • Scale meal delivery to senior centers in Bushwick, East Flatbush & Hunts Point
  • Fund financial counseling at community health centers in high-proposal neighborhoods
  • Accommodate dietary restrictions (halal, vegetarian, vegan) for equitable food access
02
Healthcare Access

Bring Healthcare to the Community

  • Fund mobile clinic units in Jamaica/SE Queens, South Bronx & Harlem
  • Launch multilingual insurance enrollment programs for uninsured residents
  • Link social services with healthcare to reduce fragmented system navigation
03
Built Environment

Invest in Age-Friendly Infrastructure

  • Mandate accessibility audits in top Built Env't proposal neighborhoods
  • Implement fall-prevention programs: home safety, exercise, sidewalk repair
  • Prioritize Hunts Point, Bushwick & Brownsville for infrastructure investment

Will anyone listen this time?

Three years of data tell a consistent and urgent story. Neighborhoods like Bushwick, Hunts Point & Longwood, and East Flatbush are not raising isolated concerns.

They are raising the same compounding needs: economic, healthcare, and environmental, year after year.

The People's Money Program gives communities a voice. The question is whether the city's leaders are prepared to act on what they hear.
